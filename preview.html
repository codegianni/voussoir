State aid in the age of industrial policy: how Brussels is rewriting its own rules

The European Commission's temporary frameworks for state aid have become structural. What this signals for competition policy and the single market.

GP
Giovanni Perrini
Policy analyst · Voussoir
March 2026·8 min read
Abstract
This analysis examines how the European Commission's successive temporary frameworks for state aid — initially designed as crisis instruments — have gradually been absorbed into the permanent architecture of EU competition policy.

The single market's competition rules were never designed for peacetime industrial policy. They emerged from a post-war consensus that subsidies distort markets, that state intervention should be the exception, and that the Commission's role was to be the guardian of level playing fields. That consensus is under revision.

Since 2020, three successive "temporary" frameworks — the Covid-19 Temporary Framework, the TCTF for energy and then for REPowerEU, and now the Temporary Crisis and Transition Framework — have accumulated a body of doctrine that is anything but temporary.1

From exception to architecture

The Temporary Crisis and Transition Framework has done something structurally significant: it has legitimised the use of state aid as a tool for strategic economic policy, not merely as a corrective instrument for market failures. This is a profound shift in the underlying logic of EU competition law.

"Each crisis framework deposits sediment that shapes the next. The temporary has become the baseline."
Commission internal note, DG Comp, 2025

The numbers bear this out. Between 2020 and 2025, approved state aid volumes in the EU averaged roughly 3.8% of GDP annually — compared to a pre-Covid decade average of around 0.7%. Even discounting the most acute crisis periods, the 2023–2025 baseline sits nearly double the historical norm.2

The Italian case

For Italy, the implications are acute. As a large economy with significant industrial legacy and chronic fiscal constraints, Italy occupies a structurally disadvantaged position in a state aid regime that rewards fiscal capacity.

Notes
1 Commission Communication C(2023) 1882, amending the Temporary Crisis and Transition Framework for state aid measures.
2 European Commission, State Aid Scoreboard 2024, DG Competition, Brussels, February 2025.
