Energy Costs and Industrial Competitiveness: Scenarios for Italy 2026–2030

Rising energy prices are eroding Italy's industrial base. This report benchmarks European compensation policies and models three policy pathways through 2030 — from targeted subsidies to accelerated green transition.

Giovanni Perrini · February 2026 · 48 pages
Download full report (PDF) Executive summary
Hero image

Context & Methodology

The European energy crisis of 2021–2023, exacerbated by geopolitical tensions and supply-chain disruptions, has created unprecedented pressure on industrial competitiveness across the EU. For Italy, the impact has been particularly acute: electricity and gas prices have risen to levels 60–80% above the pre-pandemic five-year average, significantly eroding profit margins in chemicals, metals, ceramics, and paper production. This report analyses the structural sustainability of Italy's energy-intensive sectors under prolonged high-cost scenarios.

Our methodology combines administrative microdata on firm-level energy costs, sectoral input-output analysis, and comparative econometric modelling of policy instruments deployed by peer economies. We assess three policy pathways through 2030 using a computable general equilibrium framework calibrated to post-pandemic Italian input-output tables. Uncertainty is quantified via Monte Carlo simulation across energy price, subsidy duration, and technology adoption parameters.

Key Findings

  1. 1
    Energy costs now represent 12–18% of production costs in affected sectors, up from 4–7% in 2019. Without intervention, exposed firms face cumulative margin compression of €8–12 billion annually.
  2. 2
    Germany's dual approach — targeted subsidies combined with capacity investment — has stabilised industrial output; France's price caps have constrained supply. Italy's mixed support has been less effective, leaving competitiveness gaps of 15–20% versus peer economies.
  3. 3
    Transition to renewable power could reduce long-term exposure but requires €18–24 billion capex and 5–7 years lead time. Without coordinated sectoral investment, energy-intensive manufacturing risks permanent relocation.

Impact on Italian Industry

Italy's energy-intensive sectors employ approximately 280,000 workers and generated €68 billion in annual value-added pre-crisis. Elevated energy costs have triggered a visible shift in competitive positioning, with output declining 7–13% in chemicals, 5–9% in non-metallic minerals, and 4–7% in base metals relative to 2019 baselines. Export volumes to non-EU markets have contracted as global prices remain below domestic-equivalent levels.

"Without decisive policy intervention coupled to sectoral transition investment, Italy faces a permanent shrinkage of its energy-intensive industrial base, with cascading effects on regional employment and supply-chain resilience."

Policy Benchmarking

Comparative analysis of measures adopted by France, Germany, Spain, and Poland reveals a spectrum of approaches ranging from short-term demand-side relief to supply-side investment and sectoral transition support. France's regulated price framework has preserved industrial demand but created supply-side risks and fiscal strain. Germany's mixed model — combining time-limited price support with accelerated renewable capacity deployment — appears most aligned with medium-term competitiveness objectives, though requires sustained fiscal commitment. Italy's approach has been relatively modest and fragmented across regions, limiting effectiveness.

Recommendations

  1. 1

    Adopt a sectoral transition framework

    Design a five-year integrated policy combining immediate relief for exposed firms with accelerated green capex support. Target €2–3 billion annually in transition grants and concessional financing linked to decarbonisation milestones.

  2. 2

    Expand renewable energy infrastructure

    Double renewable generation capacity by 2030 through accelerated permitting, grid investment, and industrial power purchase agreements (PPAs). Reduce deployment barriers and coordinate with national energy independence goals.

  3. 3

    Strengthen EU-level coordination

    Advocate within EU forums for harmonised energy cost relief and targeted industrial policy support. Bilateral arrangements with France and Germany on electricity exchange and investment coordination would enhance negotiating leverage.

  4. 4

    Establish foresight and monitoring mechanisms

    Create a dedicated inter-ministerial task force to track sectoral competitiveness indicators, firm-level relocation risk, and policy effectiveness. Adjust interventions quarterly based on energy market signals and employment outcomes.
